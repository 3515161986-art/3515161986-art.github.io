Figure 3: Industrial Output Index (1750-1950) - The Reversal of Fortune

This figure illustrates the reversal of fortune: countries that were rich in 1500 (e.g., India, Mexico) became relatively poorer by 1950, while countries that were poor in 1500 (e.g., United States, Canada) became richer. The reversal coincided with the Industrial Revolution.

Figure 4: Institutional Premium Before and After Industrial Revolution

The event study shows the divergence in GDP per capita between countries with "good" and "bad" institutions around the Industrial Revolution (1800). Before 1800, the gap was small and not statistically significant. After 1800, the gap widened substantially and became highly significant.

Data Sources and Variable Definitions

Core Variables:

  • Urbanization Rate (1500): Percentage of population living in cities with 5,000+ inhabitants. Data from Bairoch (1988) and others.
  • GDP per capita (1995): Log of PPP-adjusted GDP per capita. Data from World Bank (1999) World Development Indicators.
  • Institutional Quality: Property rights protection and executive constraint scores (1985-1995). Data from Political Risk Services, Polity III dataset.
  • Population Density (1500): Log population density per square kilometer of arable land. Data from Barro (1986) and McEvedy and Jones (1978).
  • Instrumental Variable: Log settler mortality rate. Data from Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson (2001a).

Time Series Data:

  • Industrial Output Index: 1750-1950, UK 1900 = 100. Data from Bairoch (1982), Chandler (1987).
  • Urbanization Rate Time Series: 800-1920. Data from Bairoch (1988).

Sample Information:

  • Full sample: 162 countries
  • Urbanization baseline sample: 41 countries
  • Population density baseline sample: 91 countries
  • Core visualization sample: 31 countries

Key Findings:

  • (1) Initial prosperity (urbanization in 1500) is negatively correlated with modern income, contrary to the geography hypothesis.
  • (2) Institutions mediate much of this relationship - the 2SLS coefficient with institutions as the endogenous variable is smaller and not significant.
  • (3) The reversal of fortune coincides with the Industrial Revolution, suggesting that geography determined institutional development which in turn affected long-run growth.
  • (4) The interaction between institutions and industrialization is positive and significant, indicating that industrialization amplified institutional differences.

Reference: Acemoglu, D., Johnson, S., & Robinson, J. A. (2002). "Reversal of Fortune: Geography and Institutions in the Making of the Modern World Income Distribution." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 117(4), 1231-1294.
